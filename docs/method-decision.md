# Method Decision

## Candidate models

Three models were evaluated because they are realistic for an adult learner on iPhone with low budget and limited daily time.

### Model A: `SRS-heavy mobile`

Core idea:

- most study time goes to flashcards, with occasional listening or speaking

Strengths:

- easy to run on a phone
- very low cost
- strong memory support for isolated items

Weaknesses:

- poor transfer to spontaneous speaking
- high risk of review backlog
- encourages recognition more than production unless tightly designed
- can feel productive without improving conversational access

### Model B: `input-heavy mobile`

Core idea:

- most study time goes to listening and reading simple Spanish, with little deliberate retrieval and delayed speaking

Strengths:

- low stress
- easy to do in public and while tired
- low backlog risk

Weaknesses:

- slower activation of known words into speech
- easy to drift into passive consumption
- weaker fit for a learner who already likes SRS and wants speaking sooner

### Model C: `balanced speaking-first mobile`

Core idea:

- `SRS` supports memory, but daily or near-daily study also includes simple input, spoken recall, and brief targeted form work

Strengths:

- best transfer to everyday speaking
- aligns with Four Strands and output research
- works in both quiet and voice modes
- less dependent on paid teaching

Weaknesses:

- requires more discipline than just doing reviews
- can feel uncomfortable at first because speaking begins early

## Rubric

Scoring is `1-5`, where `5` is best for your specific profile.

| Criterion | SRS-heavy | Input-heavy | Balanced speaking-first |
| --- | ---: | ---: | ---: |
| Transfer to daily speaking | 2 | 3 | 5 |
| Fit for `25-35 min/day` | 4 | 3 | 4 |
| Works in quiet/public mode | 5 | 5 | 4 |
| Works in voice/private mode | 2 | 2 | 5 |
| Low-budget viability | 5 | 5 | 4 |
| Motivation durability | 2 | 4 | 4 |
| Backlog risk | 1 | 5 | 4 |
| Fit with low grammar appetite | 4 | 5 | 4 |
| **Total** | **25** | **32** | **34** |

## Decision

The recommended method is:

## `Balanced speaking-first mobile system`

Why this wins:

- it preserves the part you already know you like, `SRS`
- it directly optimizes for speaking rather than just remembering
- it works on iPhone in both quiet and voice contexts
- it keeps cost low by using solo production rather than frequent tutoring
- it allows limited form work without turning the whole project into grammar study

## Required components

These parts are mandatory in the method.

### 1. `SRS` as memory engine

Rules:

- max `8-10 min/day`
- prefer cards that require recall, not just recognition
- prioritize high-frequency words, phrases, and chunks you want to say
- if backlog spikes, cut new cards before adding time

### 2. Meaning-focused input

Rules:

- max `8-10 min/day`
- choose very easy or heavily scaffolded beginner material
- favor audio with text/transcript
- reuse the same material across days instead of chasing novelty

### 3. Spoken retrieval and output

Rules:

- daily if possible, minimum `5-10 min`
- use prompted monologues, retells, question-answer drills, or AI voice turns
- target short, reusable topics: self-introduction, daily routine, likes/dislikes, plans, opinions

### 4. Just-in-time form and pronunciation

Rules:

- `2-5 min`, not a full grammar lesson
- trigger only from repeated speaking friction
- examples: ser/estar confusion, gender/article mismatch, present tense forms you keep needing, common connectors, question patterns

## Daily mobile workflow

This is the default day shape.

### Quiet/public version

Use when you are commuting, waiting, or cannot speak aloud.

1. `5-8 min` SRS review with active recall
2. `5-8 min` easy audio + transcript or short text + audio
3. `2-4 min` silent recall:
   imagine answering 2-3 prompts in Spanish before checking support

### Voice/private version

Use at home or anywhere you can speak aloud.

1. `5-8 min` SRS review
2. `5-8 min` easy audio or short dialogue
3. `8-12 min` spoken recall:
   retell, answer prompts, or use AI voice mode
4. `2-4 min` micro-fix:
   one pronunciation or form issue that blocked output

## Phrase pipeline: from input to active speech

Every useful item should move through this path:

1. `Notice`
   identify a useful high-frequency word or phrase from easy input
2. `Capture`
   store only items you can imagine saying soon
3. `Retrieve`
   review with SRS or self-test
4. `Say`
   force at least one spoken use the same day or next day
5. `Recycle`
   reuse the item in a new short answer within 48 hours

This is the key anti-passivity rule of the system.

## Tool categories needed on iPhone

The method needs these tool types, regardless of final app choices:

- an `SRS` tool with fast mobile review
- a voice capture or voice memo tool
- a simple AI or prompt system for short speaking turns
- beginner-friendly audio/text materials
- a low-friction capture inbox for new phrases

Examples of acceptable built-in iPhone functions:

- Voice Memos for spoken output and self-review
- Notes or Reminders as capture inboxes
- Shortcuts only if they reduce friction instead of adding setup overhead

## What not to do

Do not build the method around these patterns:

- more than `10 min/day` of review by default
- adding lots of new cards while reviews are already piling up
- watching difficult native content too early
- delaying speaking until you "know enough"
- separate grammar study blocks longer than the speaking block
- chasing streaks or app engagement as if they were proof of spoken ability
