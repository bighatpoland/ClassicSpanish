# Research Synthesis

## Research question

What is the most evidence-aligned way for an adult learner to improve spoken Spanish on an iPhone with `25-35 min/day`, low budget, a preference for `SRS`, and a dislike of grammar-heavy study?

## High-confidence takeaways

### 1. Spacing and retrieval should be part of the core system

The evidence for distributed practice and retrieval practice is strong across learning contexts. For this project, the important implication is not just "use flashcards", but "retrieve actively, repeatedly, and over time".

Why it matters here:

- `SRS` is a good fit for your preferences
- retrieval is more useful than rereading or passive exposure
- the main danger is over-allocating time to reviews and starving speaking practice

Sources:

- Cepeda et al. (2006), meta-analysis of distributed practice: spacing and final retention interact, and longer retention goals require wider spacing.  
  https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/16719566/
- Dunlosky (2013), overview of effective learning techniques: practice testing and distributed practice were rated the most effective of the reviewed strategies.  
  https://www.aft.org/ae/fall2013/dunlosky

### 2. SRS alone is not enough if the real goal is speaking

Vocabulary review helps memory, but a speaking goal needs a broader system. Paul Nation's "Four Strands" framework is the clearest fit here: balance meaning-focused input, meaning-focused output, language-focused learning, and fluency development.

Why it matters here:

- if `SRS` becomes most of the study time, the plan becomes memory training instead of speaking training
- the right role for SRS is "memory engine", not "whole curriculum"
- a mobile plan should still include input, output, and fluency practice every week

Sources:

- Nation (2007), "The four strands"  
  https://openaccess.wgtn.ac.nz/articles/journal_contribution/The_four_strands/12552167
- Nation (2025-hosted PDF of practical summary), "What do you need to know to learn a foreign language?"  
  https://www.wgtn.ac.nz/lals/resources/paul-nations-resources/paul-nations-publications/publications/documents/foreign-language_1125.pdf

### 3. Speaking practice should start early, even if it is imperfect and solo

The output literature does not say input is unimportant. It says output adds something different: it exposes gaps, forces retrieval, and helps learners notice what they cannot yet say.

Why it matters here:

- waiting to speak "until ready" is likely to slow down speaking development
- solo spoken recall, prompted monologues, and short voice conversations are useful even before live tutoring
- the first target is not elegance; it is access to simple meaning under light pressure

Sources:

- Swain-related overview via Cambridge: output contributes to learning, not just fluency, and learners benefit when production pushes them slightly beyond current comfort.  
  https://www.cambridge.org/core/books/teaching-languages-to-adolescent-learners/opportunities-for-language-output/6998EEAEE5B72FD224C3112BEB8BBD85
- Swain interview summary: output helps learners notice gaps, test hypotheses, and reflect on form while producing language.  
  https://periodicos.ufpel.edu.br/ojs2/index.php/interfaces/article/view/18775

### 4. Small, focused form work helps adults, but grammar should stay narrow

The strongest interpretation of the instruction literature is not "study lots of grammar first". It is closer to: explicit attention can help, especially when it is focused and tied to real performance problems.

Why it matters here:

- a no-grammar-ever plan would likely leave recurring speaking errors unresolved
- a grammar-heavy plan would violate your preferences and probably reduce consistency
- the best fit is `just-in-time form`: small, reactive fixes for recurring problems

Source:

- Norris and Ortega (2000/2002 listing), meta-analysis summary: focused L2 instruction produced substantial gains, and explicit instruction outperformed implicit instruction in the reviewed studies.  
  https://www.researchgate.net/publication/228003219_Effectiveness_of_L2_Instruction_A_Research_Synthesis_and_Quantitative_Meta-analysis

Inference from source:

The source does not imply that explicit grammar should dominate your schedule. It supports keeping a narrow lane for targeted explanations when they unblock performance.

### 5. Pronunciation work should target intelligibility, not native-like accent

For a speaking-first learner on low budget, aiming at comprehensibility is more realistic and more useful than chasing a native accent.

Why it matters here:

- your pronunciation practice should focus on being understood
- this favors short listen-repeat, shadowing, chunk imitation, and feedback loops
- it does not require perfectionist correction of every sound

Source:

- ELT Journal overview of intelligibility work, drawing on Derwing and Munro: intelligibility concerns whether the message is understood, while accentedness is a different construct.  
  https://academic.oup.com/eltj/article/69/2/202/408492

### 6. Mobile learning works best as short, repeated, multimodal learning

Recent MALL reviews suggest mobile learning helps vocabulary and pronunciation, learners tend to respond positively, and smartphones are central devices. The research also suggests that mobile features are most useful when they support meaningful learning rather than endless low-value tapping.

Why it matters here:

- the phone is a valid primary environment
- mobile learning is especially well suited to short review, capture, listen-repeat, and prompted speaking
- the mobile workflow still needs pedagogical discipline; app engagement is not the same as language progress

Sources:

- Okumuş Dağdeler (2023), systematic review of mobile-assisted vocabulary learning research  
  https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s40561-023-00235-z
- Metruk (2024), systematic review of MALL and pronunciation instruction: overall beneficial effects and positive learner attitudes; smartphones were the most prevalent devices in the reviewed studies.  
  https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10639-024-12453-0

## Expert-derived design rules for this project

These are the practical rules that survive the literature review:

1. Use `retrieval + spacing`, not rereading, as the main memory mechanism.
2. Keep `SRS` capped so it supports speaking instead of replacing speaking.
3. Start speaking now, even if the early output is short, slow, and solo.
4. Work with high-frequency words and reusable chunks before abstract vocabulary.
5. Use a dual mobile workflow: `quiet/public` and `voice/private`.
6. Add micro-grammar only when it fixes a repeated speaking problem.
7. Train pronunciation for intelligibility and rhythm, not accent perfection.

## What this means for the next stage

The evidence does not support a pure `SRS-only` system. It supports a `balanced speaking-first mobile system` with a strict time cap on reviews, regular simple input, and daily speaking production in some form.
